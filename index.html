At the time everyone was worried about the 2016 US election. I figured that everything would be fine. Trump didn't have a hope of winning.

Meanwhile, Sasjkia was thinking about civil rights issues like

Voter rights, overturning of the Voting Rights Act, disenfranchisement, voter ID laws, gerrymandering, Citizens United, Super PACs.

Sasjkia was thinking about ways that we can help improve voter turnout.

She asked me, what if there were a website that helped connect people with cars with people who need a ride to the polls? Asked me what I thought.

I was sceptical about the idea. I thought that if local organisers weren't already doing this then there wasn't a demand.

I was wrong on both counts.

Sasjkia went out and talked to organisations like Rock the Vote, AAPD, NAACP, and People Demanding Action.

She told them she had this idea and knew people with the skills to get it done. They said "yeah that sounds like a great idea"!

Just because it hasn't already been done, doesn't mean it's not a good idea.

This may seem obvious to other people.
I tend to assume that if something hasn't been done already, it's probably because it's not a good idea. This is true for most things, like, idk, unicorn pants. But not everything.
I was busy so I left them to it but told Sasjkia I'd help out at some point.
Had a free Friday night, figured I'd help out with a tiny issue or two. Found two major branches, each wildly different. Unfinished redesign branch, and people were still committing on master. Before I could tackle any tasks, someone needed to finish the redesign that Jo had started, and fix merge conflicts. So that was my weekend gone.
I've been working on it ever since, because someone needed to, and now I'm de facto front-end lead I guess?

People drop in and out. Less reliable. Harder to fill certain roles.

Open source means you don't get to pick your colleagues, you take what you can get. Many contributions are great, some not so much. Some are like a cat just gave you a gift of a dead bird. (e.g. the PHP captcha)

That being said, we've had some really valuable contributions from junior devs, I LOVE getting junior dev contributions and we really want more. The problem isn't bad code, it's people who don't read the instructions properly. I hate having to turn away someone's hard work.

Time zones are hard

Our users are in different US States, and enter their availability. The US covers several time zones so we wanted to use absolute times and account for time zone differences.

Dealing with times and zones in JavaScript is a nightmare.
